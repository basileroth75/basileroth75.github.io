The Krill Story
01 A keystone the size of a paperclip ▾

Krill are barely the length of a paperclip, but the entire Southern Ocean is built on them. This shrimp-like crustacean is a keystone species for the food web: it feeds whales, seals, penguins, and seabirds across the entire ecosystem. And yet, every year, industrial trawlers descend on this remote ecosystem to harvest it by the hundreds of thousands of tons, led overwhelmingly by the Norwegian company Aker BioMarine. With its two trawlers under the Norwegian flag, Antarctic Endurance and Saga Sea, it vacuums out as much as 62% of the total yearly krill catch.

But most of what's caught doesn't feed people. It becomes aquaculture feed for farmed fish on the other side of the planet, oil supplements, and pet food. Aker BioMarine is even the recipient of millions of dollars in subsidies from the Norwegian government in order to remain competitive.

02 Where the fishing concentrates ▾

Zoom in, and the impact of that extraction comes into focus. Here, fishing is governed by the Commission for the Conservation of Antarctic Marine Living Resources (CCAMLR), which set an overall catch trigger of 620,000 tonnes for krill. Aker BioMarine claims that, since it's a small fraction of total estimated krill biomass, it's proof the fishery is sustainable. But total catch limits obscure localized harm.

Fishing vessels cluster exactly where krill, and the marine predators that depend on it, are most concentrated: the western Antarctic Peninsula, the South Orkney Islands, and South Georgia. In 2022, catch concentration there reached its highest level in 38 years (Cruz et al., 2022), and local catch can now rival or exceed what marine predators need, and even the standing stock of krill itself (Trathan et al., 2021). This is in addition to the already severe impacts of climate change, disrupting the Antarctic climate and the delicate balance of its food webs. Calling a regional catch quota "sustainable" is meaningless if the harvest is happening in the one place the ecosystem is least resilient to it.

03 A fix that's already on the table ▾

This crisis has a fix, and it's already been sitting on the table. It's spatialization: dividing the fishing quota across sub-regions to spread out fishing pressure. CCAMLR used to do just this, until member states failed to renew the rule in 2024. The industry's answer was to accelerate. In 2025, for the first time, the entire 620,000-tonne limit could be taken from anywhere within the zone, and the fishery shut down early from the resulting hyper-concentration of catch from Aker BioMarine and others. This year again, the fishing quota has been met faster than ever.

Meanwhile, the Domain 1 Marine Protected Area, a proposed no-take zone around the Antarctic Peninsula where fishing pressure currently concentrates, has been repeatedly blocked for nearly a decade.

Adopting spatialization measures and the Domain 1 marine protected area are two sides of the same coin: an Antarctic krill fishery held to the limits of what the ecosystem will bear. What's clear is who is benefiting from this: a handful of companies, Aker BioMarine foremost among them, are extracting a keystone species for economic benefit, so it can end up in a salmon pellet or supplement capsule.
